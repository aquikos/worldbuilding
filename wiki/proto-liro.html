Proto-Daimarian is the ancestor language of the languages found on the Daimarian peninsula, which includes languages such as Liró. Proto-Daimarian's most notable feature is the extensive case system, with 13 cases, and 2 numbers, which combine for 26 separate forms for every noun. The verb system is incredibly complex, marking three tenses, five aspects, four main moods, person, and number. This produces a very large amount of conjugations for each verb, but most have verb regular constructions, easing the process significantly.

Phonology

The phonology of Proto-Daimarian may be difficult for speakers of many languages, due to both an aspiration distinction, and a voicing distinction. There is also a /r/ /l/ distinction, and initial position /ŋ/.

Orthography

For the most part, the orthography of Proto-Liro is fairly straightforward, though a few phonemes pose issues. Apart from the aspirated series, /x/, and /ɣ/, all phonemes are written as they are in the IPA. Aspirated consonants are simply written with an h following the letter, meaning /pʰ/ is written ⟨ph⟩. Because ⟨gh⟩ is taken by the aspirated voiced velar plosive, /x/ and /ɣ/ are transcribed as ⟨h⟩ and ⟨x⟩ respectively.

Grammar

On the most basic level, Proto-Daimarian had SVO word order, which is familiar to speakers of most European languages. Apart from demonstratives, almost all modifiers go after the noun. Same applies to verbs, where adverbs are placed after the verb. This is largely where it stops being easy.

Proto-Daimarian had an extensive case system, which survives in many of its daughter languages. Each case has a singular, base form and a plural form, which is fairly straightforward.

Proto-Daimarian had a very complex verb system. Tense, aspect, and mood are all marked directly on the verb. Fortunately, there is only the active voice. Unfortunately, person and number is also marked on the verb. This leads to rather long verb constructions, which were heavily reduced in most descending languages. There are three tenses, past, present, and future, five aspects, simple, imperfective, perfective, stative, and progressive, four grammaticalized moods, indicative, imperative, optative, and conditional. The three persons, and two numbers are both marked on the verb, producing six different conjugations.
