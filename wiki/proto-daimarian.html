Proto-Daimarian is the ancestor language of the languages found on the Daimarian peninsula, which includes languages such as Liró. For the most part, it is a fairly straightforward language. Some learners may find the case system to be difficult, and some may find the verb system the same. The phonology may also be difficult, but is simple once it is learned.

Phonology

The biggest issue for learners would likely be the ejective series, but outside of that it is fairly straightforward.

Orthography

The orthography of Proto-Daimarian is fairly straightforward. Almost all consonants are represented by their Latin equivalents. The primary exceptions are the ejective series, the aspirated plosive series, /ɣ/, and /ŋ/. Ejectives are represented by the voiceless plosive symbol for that place of articulation, and ' after, so /pʼ/ would be ⟨p'⟩. Because no technical distinction is made, the aspirated series is simply the voiceless plosive symbol for that place of articulation. The voiced velar plosive and nasal are represented by ⟨gh⟩ and ⟨nh⟩, as ⟨h⟩ is not used for anything else.

Phonotactics

Proto-Daimarian is purely CV(C). Consonants always appear initially, and may appear finally. Aspirated consonants cannot appear finally.

Grammar

On the most basic level, Proto-Daimarian has SVO word order(by default), which is familiar to speakers of many languages. Apart from demonstratives(and articles), all modifiers go after the noun.

Nouns

The case system of Proto-Daimarian is significantly simpler than many of its descendents, having only 5 cases. There are only two grammatical numbers, and articles are not declined for case or number.
